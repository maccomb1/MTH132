    Section 3.5 Mean Value Theorem

    Objectives

    • State Rolle’s Theorem and the Mean Value Theorem, including all hypotheses.
      🔗
    • Interpret the Mean Value Theorem geometrically as a tangent line parallel to a secant line.
      🔗
    • Solve for values of \(c\) that satisfies the Mean Value Theorem in selected examples.
      🔗
    🔗
    Derivatives tell us the instantaneous rate of change of a function. In this section, we connect derivatives to the overall behavior of a graph on an interval. The key message is that somewhere between two points, the instantaneous rate of change must match the average rate of change.
    🔗

    Subsection Rolle’s Theorem

    We begin with a special case where the function starts and ends at the same height. In that situation, the graph must have a horizontal tangent somewhere in between.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.5.1. Rolle’s Theorem.

    Let \(f\) be continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\text{.}\) If \(f(a)=f(b)\text{,}\) then there exists a number \(c\) in \((a,b)\) such that \(f'(c)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Since \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\text{,}\) the Extreme Value Theorem guarantees that \(f\) has an absolute maximum value and an absolute minimum value on \([a,b]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If a maximum (or minimum) occurs at an interior point \(c\in(a,b)\text{,}\) then \(f\) has a local extremum there. Since \(f\) is differentiable at \(c\text{,}\) the derivative must satisfy \(f'(c)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If instead both the maximum and minimum occur only at endpoints, then the condition \(f(a)=f(b)\) forces the maximum value and minimum value to be the same. In that case, \(f\) must be constant on \([a,b]\text{,}\) and therefore \(f'(x)=0\) for every \(x\in(a,b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In all cases, there exists \(c\in(a,b)\) such that \(f'(c)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.5.3.

    In mathematics, the hypotheses of a theorem are the assumptions (the “if” part), and the conclusion is what we deduce from them (the “then” part). For Rolle’s Theorem, the hypotheses are continuity on \([a,b]\text{,}\) differentiability on \((a,b)\text{,}\) and the condition \(f(a)=f(b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    A physical interpretation can help. If \(f(t)\) represents the height of a rocket that starts and ends on its launch pad, then at some moment the rocket must have zero vertical velocity. That is exactly the conclusion \(f'(c)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Remark 3.5.4.

    Each hypothesis matters. If a function is not differentiable (for example, it has a corner), then the conclusion can fail. A graph can rise and then fall sharply without ever having a well-defined horizontal tangent.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.5.6.

    Consider the function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=x^2 -4x+3 \end{equation*}
    Verify that the hypotheses and conclusion of Rolle’s Theorem on the interval \([0,4]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The function \(f\) is a polynomial, so it is continuous on \([0,4]\) and differentiable on \((0,4)\text{.}\) Also \(f(0)=3\) and \(f(4)=3\text{,}\) so the hypotheses of Rolle’s Theorem are satisfied. Differentiating gives \(f'(x)=2x-4\text{.}\) Setting this equal to zero gives \(x=2\text{,}\) which is in the interval \((0,4)\text{.}\) Therefore there exists \(c=2\) such that \(f'(c)=0\text{,}\) as Rolle’s Theorem predicts.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Derivatives versus Difference Quotients

    Throughout our theory, the derivative \(f'(a)\) has been shadowed by the difference quotient \(\frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\text{,}\) over some interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) Numerically, the difference quotient is an approximation to the derivative \(\frac{df}{dx} \approx \frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x}\text{,}\) provided \(\Delta x\) is small. In physical terms, the difference quotient is the average rate of change of \(f(x)\) over \(x \in [a,b]\text{.}\) Geometrically, in terms of the graph \(y = f(x)\text{,}\) the difference quotient is the slope of the secant line cutting through the points \((a,f(a))\) and \((b,f(b))\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now we come to the basic theorem that connects the derivative of a function with its basic properties. It says that the derivative is somewhere exactly equal to the difference quotient, that is, the slope of one particular tangent line is equal to the slope of the secant line.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.5.7. Mean Value Theorem.

    Let \(f\) be continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\text{.}\) Then there exists a number \(c\) in \((a,b)\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Proof.

    The proof uses Rolle’s Theorem by subtracting off the secant line. This produces a new function whose endpoints match, so Rolle’s Theorem applies.
    🔗
    Assume \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\text{.}\) Define a new function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} g(x)=f(x)-\left(f(a)+\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(x-a)\right). \end{equation*}
    The function \(g\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\text{,}\) since it is built from \(f\) and linear functions. Also,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} g(a)=f(a)-\left(f(a)+\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(a-a)\right)=0, \end{equation*}
    and similarly \(g(b)=0\text{.}\) Therefore \(g(a)=g(b)\text{,}\) and Rolle’s Theorem applies. So there exists \(c\in(a,b)\) such that \(g'(c)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Differentiate \(g\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} g'(x)=f'(x)-\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}. \end{equation*}
    Substituting \(x=c\) and using \(g'(c)=0\) gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0=f'(c)-\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}, \end{equation*}
    which is equivalent to
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Remark 3.5.9.

    Rolle’s Theorem is the special case of the Mean Value Theorem when \(f(a)=f(b)\text{.}\) In that case, the secant slope is \(0\text{,}\) so the conclusion becomes \(f'(c)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.5.10.

    The Mean Value Theorem guarantees that at least one such value of \(c\) exists, but it does not provide a direct method to find it. In many applications, we only need to know that some \(c\) exists. When practice problems ask you to find \(c\text{,}\) we usually solve the equation \(f'(x)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.5.11.

    Let \(f(x)=5\sqrt{x}-x\sqrt{x}\) on the interval \([0,4]\text{.}\) Verify that the hypotheses of the Mean Value Theorem apply, and find all values of \(c\) that satisfy the conclusion.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The function \(\sqrt{x}\) is continuous for \(x\ge 0\text{,}\) so \(f\) is continuous on \([0,4]\text{.}\) Differentiating (for \(x\gt 0\)),
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x)=\left(5x^{1/2}-x^{3/2}\right)'=\frac{5}{2}x^{-1/2}-\frac{3}{2}x^{1/2}. \end{equation*}
    This derivative exists for every \(x\) in \((0,4)\text{,}\) so the Mean Value Theorem applies. Compute the average rate of change:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f(4)-f(0)}{4-0}=\frac{\left(5\cdot 2-4\cdot 2\right)-0}{4}=\frac{2}{4}=\frac{1}{2}. \end{equation*}
    We solve \(f'(c)=\frac{1}{2}\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{5}{2}c^{-1/2}-\frac{3}{2}c^{1/2}=\frac{1}{2}. \end{equation*}
    Multiply both sides by \(2\sqrt{c}\) (valid since \(c>0\)):
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 5-3c=\sqrt{c}. \end{equation*}
    Let \(u=\sqrt{c}\text{,}\) so \(c=u^2\text{.}\) Then the equation becomes
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 5-3u^2=u \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad 3u^2+u-5=0. \end{equation*}
    Using the quadratic formula,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} u=\frac{-1\pm\sqrt{1+60}}{6}=\frac{-1\pm\sqrt{61}}{6}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(u=\sqrt{c}>0\text{,}\) we take the positive root: \(u=\frac{\sqrt{61}-1}{6}\text{.}\) Therefore
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} c=u^2=\left(\frac{\sqrt{61}-1}{6}\right)^2\approx 1.29. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Derivative Controls Direction

    One of the first important consequences of the Mean Value Theorem it allows us to prove some intuition we have about how the sign of the derivative controls whether a function increases or decreases. Intuitively if the slope of the tangent line is positive, then the function should be increasing. The Mean Value Theorem allows us to make this intuition precise and rigorous. This will become increasingly important as we move into the next sections on curve sketching and optimization.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.5.13. Positive Derivative Implies Increasing.

    Suppose \(f\) is differentiable on an interval \(I\text{.}\) If \(f'(x)\gt 0\) for every \(x\in I\text{,}\) then \(f\) is increasing on \(I\text{.}\) That is, whenever \(a \lt b\) in \(I\text{,}\) we have \(f(a) \lt f(b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(a \lt b\) be any two points in \(I\text{.}\) By the Mean Value Theorem, there exists \(c\in(a,b)\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(b-a \gt 0\) and \(f'(c)\gt 0\text{,}\) we conclude \(f(b)-f(a)\gt 0\text{,}\) so \(f(b)\gt f(a)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.5.14.

    A similar argument shows that if \(f'(x)<0\) throughout an interval, then \(f\) is decreasing on that interval.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Exercises Practice / Study Problems

    Some True/False Problems.

    Determine whether each statement is true or false and explain your reasoning.
    🔗
    1.
    If a function is continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\text{,}\) then there exists exactly one number \(c\) satisfying the Mean Value Theorem.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The Mean Value Theorem guarantees at least one such \(c\text{,}\) but there may be more than one. For example, for \(f(x)=x^3\) on \([-1,1]\text{,}\) both \(c=-\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\) and \(c=\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\) satisfy the conclusion.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    If \(f(a)=f(b)\) and \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) but not differentiable at some point in \((a,b)\text{,}\) then Rolle’s Theorem still guarantees a value \(c\) such that \(f'(c)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Rolle’s Theorem requires differentiability on the entire open interval \((a,b)\text{.}\) If there is a point where the function is not differentiable, then the conclusion can fail. For example, for \(f(x)=|x|\) on \([-1,1]\text{,}\) we have \(f(-1)=f(1)\text{,}\) and \(f\) is continuous on \([-1,1]\text{,}\) but there is no value of \(c\in(-1,1)\) such that \(f'(c)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    If \(f'(x)=0\) for every \(x\) in \((a,b)\text{,}\) then \(f(a)=f(b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. By the Mean Value Theorem, there exists \(c\in(a,b)\) such that \(f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\text{.}\) Since \(f'(c)=0\text{,}\) we have \(\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}=0\text{,}\) which implies \(f(b)-f(a)=0\text{,}\) so \(f(a)=f(b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    4.
    The Mean Value Theorem guarantees that the tangent line at \(x=c\) passes through the points \((a,f(a))\) and \((b,f(b))\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The Mean Value Theorem guarantees that the tangent line at \(x=c\) is parallel to the secant line through \((a,f(a))\) and \((b,f(b))\text{,}\) but it does not guarantee that the tangent line passes through those points.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    5.
    If \(f\) is differentiable on \((a,b)\) and continuous on \([a,b]\text{,}\) then there exists \(c\in(a,b)\) such that \(f'(c)=\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. This is exactly the statement of the Mean Value Theorem.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    6.
    If \(f'(x) \lt 0\) for all \(x\) in \((a,b)\) where \(a \lt b\text{,}\) then \(f(b) \lt f(a)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. By the Mean Value Theorem, there exists \(c\in(a,b)\) such that \(f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\text{.}\) Since \(b-a \gt 0\) and \(f'(c) \lt 0\text{,}\) we have \(f(b)-f(a) \lt 0\text{,}\) which implies \(f(b) \lt f(a)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    7.
    For \(f(x)=x^2\) on \([1,3]\text{,}\) there exists \(c\) such that \(f'(c)=4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. The function \(f\) is a polynomial, so it is continuous on \([1,3]\) and differentiable on \((1,3)\text{.}\) Compute the average rate of change:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f(3)-f(1)}{3-1}=\frac{9-1}{2}=4. \end{equation*}
    By the Mean Value Theorem, there exists \(c\in(1,3)\) such that \(f'(c)=4\text{.}\) In fact, since \(f'(x)=2x\text{,}\) we can solve \(2c=4\) to find \(c=2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    8.
    The Mean Value Theorem applies to every polynomial on any closed interval.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Polynomials are continuous and differentiable everywhere, so the Mean Value Theorem applies on any closed interval.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    9.
    If \(f'(c)=\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\text{,}\) then \(c\) must be the midpoint of \([a,b]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The Mean Value Theorem guarantees the existence of some \(c\in(a,b)\) such that \(f'(c)=\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\text{,}\) but it does not guarantee that \(c\) is the midpoint. In many cases, \(c\) is not the midpoint. For example see Example 3.5.11
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    10.
    For \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\) on \([0,4]\text{,}\) the Mean Value Theorem applies.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. The function \(\sqrt{x}\) is continuous for \(x\ge 0\text{,}\) so it is continuous on \([0,4]\text{.}\) Also, the derivative \(\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\) exists for every \(x\in(0,4)\text{,}\) so the Mean Value Theorem applies.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Algebraic and Procedural Problems.

    11.
    Let \(f(x)=x^2+1\) on \([0,3]\text{.}\) Find all values \(c\) in \((0,3)\) that satisfy the Mean Value Theorem conclusion.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Compute the secant slope:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f(3)-f(0)}{3-0}=\frac{(10)-(1)}{3}=3. \end{equation*}
    Since \(f'(x)=2x\text{,}\) solve \(2c=3\text{,}\) so \(c=\frac{3}{2}\text{.}\)

    12.
    Consider \(f(x)=(x-1)^2(x-3)\) on \([1,3]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (a) Verify that Rolle’s Theorem applies.
    🔗
    (b) Find a value \(c\) in \((1,3)\) such that \(f'(c)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The function is a polynomial, so it is continuous on \([1,3]\) and differentiable on \((1,3)\text{.}\) Also \(f(1)=0\) and \(f(3)=0\text{,}\) so Rolle’s Theorem applies.
    🔗
    Differentiate:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-1)^2(x-3) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad f'(x)=2(x-1)(x-3)+(x-1)^2. \end{equation*}
    Factor:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x)=(x-1)\bigl(2(x-3)+(x-1)\bigr)=(x-1)(3x-7). \end{equation*}
    In \((1,3)\text{,}\) the root is \(c=\frac{7}{3}\text{.}\)

    13.
    A differentiable function \(f\) satisfies \(f(2)=1\) and \(f(6)=9\text{.}\) Explain what the Mean Value Theorem guarantees about the value of \(f'(c)\) for some \(c\in(2,6)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The secant slope is \(\frac{9-1}{6-2}=2\text{.}\) The Mean Value Theorem guarantees there exists \(c\in(2,6)\) such that \(f'(c)=2\text{.}\)
