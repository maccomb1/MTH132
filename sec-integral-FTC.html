    Section 4.4 The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

    Objectives

    • State and apply the First Fundamental Theorem of Calculus to compute derivatives of functions defined by integrals.
      🔗
    • Interpret accumulation functions \(\displaystyle F(x)=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt\) as total accumulated (signed) change from \(a\) to \(x\) and recognize them as antiderivatives.
      🔗
    • Use the relationship \(F'(x)=f(x)\) to analyze accumulation functions, including determining where they are increasing, decreasing, concave up, and concave down.
      🔗
    • State and apply the Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus to evaluate definite integrals using antiderivatives.
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Introduction to the Integral as an Antiderivative

    In the previous section we used a velocity function \(v(t)\) to compute the change in position (displacement) over a time interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) The distance is an integral defined as a limit of Riemann sums, adding up (velocity)\(\times\)(time) over many small time increments:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \text{displacement} \amp = \amp s(b)-s(a)\\[0.8em] \amp = \amp \displaystyle\lim_{\Delta t\to 0} (v(t_1)\Delta t + v(t_2)\Delta t + \cdots + v(t_n)\Delta t) \\ \amp = \amp \displaystyle\int_a^b v(t)\,dt \end{array} \end{equation*}
    If we fix the starting time \(a\) and assume \(s(a)=0\text{,}\) then taking a variable endpoint \(b=x\) gives a function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} s(x) \ =\ \int_a^x v(t)\,dt. \end{equation*}
    Since the rate of change of position is velocity, \(s'(x)=v(x)\text{.}\) The remarkable point is that the integral function \(\displaystyle \int_a^x v(t)\,dt\) always produces an antiderivative of \(v\text{,}\) even if we cannot find an antiderivative by reversing derivative formulas.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection The First Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

    Theorem 4.4.1. First Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

    Let \(f\) be continuous on \([a,b]\) and define
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} F(x)\ =\ \int_a^x f(t)\,dt. \end{equation*}
    Then \(F'(x)=f(x)\) for \(x\in(a,b)\text{.}\) In particular, \(F\) is the unique antiderivative of \(f\) satisfying \(F(a)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Idea of a Proof.

    We start from the definition of the derivative:
    \begin{equation*} F'(x)=\lim_{h\to 0}\dfrac{F(x+h)-F(x)}{h} \end{equation*}
    Using the splitting rule for integrals,
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^{x+h} f(t) ~dt =\int_a^x f(t) ~dt +\int_x^{x+h}f(t) ~dt \end{equation*}
    So we have:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{F(x+h)-F(x)}{h} &=& \displaystyle \frac{1}{h}\left(\int_a^{x+h} f(t)\,dt-\int_a^x f(t)\,dt\right) \\[0.8em] &=& \displaystyle \frac{1}{h}\left(\int_a^x f(t) ~dt +\int_x^{x+h}f(t) ~dt -\int_a^x f(t)\,dt\right) \\[0.8em] &=& \displaystyle \frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Here is a sketch of the graph of \(f\) to illustrate the idea:
    🔗
    Geometrically, the integral \(\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt\) is the area under the curve of \(f\) from \(x\) to \(x+h\) (shaded in blue above). When \(h\) is small, this area is approximately a rectangle with width \(h\) and height \(f(x)\) (the hatched rectangle in the image above). So therefore
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt &\approx& \displaystyle \frac{1}{h}\cdot (f(x)\cdot h) \ =\ f(x). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    As \(h\to 0\text{,}\) the approximation becomes exact, so \(F'(x)=f(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For those worried that this is not a rigorous proof, please see the "Finer Details of the Proof" below.
    🔗
    🔗

    Finer Details of the Proof.

    In the proof idea, we used the approximation that \(\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt\approx f(x)\cdot h\) when \(h\) is small. But how do we know that the limit of the approximation is correct?
    🔗
    To control the errors, let’s take the absolute minimum value \(N\) and absolte maximum value \(M\) of the continuous function \(f\) on the closed interval \([x,x+h]\) (we can do this by the Extreme Value Theorem). To indicate that these values depend on \(h\text{,}\) we write \(N_h\) and \(M_h\text{.}\) Therefore
    \begin{equation*} N_h\le f(t)\le M_h, \quad \text{ for } t\in [x,x+h] \end{equation*}
    So the bounds rules for definite integrals give
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle N_h\le \amp f(t) \amp \le M_h \\ N_h((x+h)-x) \le \amp \displaystyle\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt \amp \le M_h ((x+h)-x) \\ N_h(h) \le \amp \displaystyle\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt \amp \le M_h (h) \\ N_h \le \amp \dfrac{1}{h}\displaystyle\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt \amp \le M_h \end{array} \end{equation*}
    As \(h\to 0\text{,}\) the interval shrinks to the point \(x\text{,}\) so by continuity
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{h\to 0}N_h=\lim_{h\to 0}M_h=f(x) \end{equation*}
    By the squeeze theorem,
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt = \lim_{h\to 0}N_h=\lim_{h\to 0}M_h=f(x)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    hence \(F'(x)=f(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The condition \(F(a)=\int_a^a f(t)\,dt=0\) and the antiderivative uniqueness theorem give the uniqueness statement.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 4.4.2.

    In physical language: the rate of change of a cumulative effect up to time \(x\) is the strength of the effect at time \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 4.4.3.

    The First Fundamental Theorem can be used as a basic derivative rule: if \(f\) is continuous, then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}\left(\int_a^x f(t)\,dt\right) \ =\ f(x). \end{equation*}
    Here \(a\) is a constant, \(x\) is the input, and \(t\) is a dummy variable that only matters inside the integral.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.4.4.

    Find the derivative of \(\displaystyle F(x)=\int_{5}^{x}\sin^4(t)\,dt\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    By the First Fundamental Theorem, \(F'(x)=\sin^4(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Note that we never needed to evaluate the integral or find an explicit formula for \(F(x)\text{.}\) The First Fundamental Theorem allows us to find the derivative without needing an explicit formula for \(F\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Accumulation Functions

    The First Fundamental Theorem of Calculus tells us that integrals with a variable upper limit naturally produce antiderivatives. Because of this, functions of the form
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} F(x)=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt \end{equation*}
    appear frequently in calculus. Such functions are called accumulation functions because they measure the total accumulated change of the quantity \(f(t)\) from \(t=a\) up to \(t=x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    As we found in our last section, the value of \(\displaystyle F(x)=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt\) represents the signed area between the graph of \(y=f(t)\) and the \(t\)-axis from \(a\) to \(x\text{.}\) Areas above the axis contribute positively, while areas below the axis contribute negatively.
    🔗
    Let’s get some more practice with accumulation functions and their relationship to the original function.
    🔗

    Example 4.4.5.

    Suppose the graph of \(g(t)\) is shown below. And let \(A(x)=\displaystyle \int_0^x g(t)\,dt.\)
    🔗
    Use the graph to answer the following questions.
    🔗
    (a) Find \(A(4) - A(3)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) Find \(A'(2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (c) On which intervals is \(A(x)\) increasing?
    🔗
    (d) On which intervals is \(A(x)\) decreasing?
    🔗
    (e) Where does \(A(x)\) have a local maximum?
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) By the definition of \(A\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} A(4)-A(3) = \int_0^4 g(t)\,dt - \int_0^3 g(t)\,dt = \int_3^4 g(t)\,dt. \end{equation*}
    The integral from 3 to 4 is the area of the green shaded region, which is 2.3. So \(A(4)-A(3)=2.3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) By the First Fundamental Theorem, \(A'(2)=g(2)\text{.}\) From the graph, \(g(2)=-2\text{,}\) so \(A'(2)=-2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (c) The function \(A(x)\) is increasing where \(A'(x)=g(x)\gt 0\text{.}\) From the graph, this happens on the intervals \((0,1)\) and \((3,5)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (d) The function \(A(x)\) is decreasing where \(A'(x)=g(x)\lt 0\text{.}\) From the graph, this happens on the interval \((1,3)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (e) Local maxima of \(A(x)\) occur where \(A'(x)=g(x)\) changes from positive to negative. From the graph, this happens at \(x=1\text{,}\) so \(A(x)\) has a local maximum at \(x=1\text{.}\) We can also check the endpoints. Since \(g(x)\gt 0\) for \(x\in(0,1)\text{,}\) \(A(x)\) is increasing on \((0,1)\text{,}\) so \(x=0\) is a local minimum. Since \(g(x)\gt 0\) for \(x\in(3,5)\text{,}\) \(A(x)\) is increasing on \((3,5)\text{,}\) so \(x=5\) is a local maximum. Therefore the local maxima of \(A(x)\) are at \(x=1\) and \(x=5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Thanks to the First Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, we can also do curve sketching style problems with accumulation functions.
    🔗

    Example 4.4.7.

    Consider the function \(H(x)=\displaystyle\int_0^x (1-t)e^t \,dt\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (a) Evaluate \(H(0)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) Find where \(H(x)\) is increasing and where it is decreasing.
    🔗
    (c) Find where \(H(x)\) is concave up and where it is concave down.
    🔗
    (d) Use the information from parts (a)-(c) to find a rough sketch the graph of \(y=H(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Part (a):
    🔗
    \(H(0)=\int_0^0 (1-t)e^t\,dt=0\) Since the lower and upper bound of the integral are the same.
    🔗
    Part (b):
    🔗
    By the First Fundamental Theorem,
    \begin{equation*} H'(x)=(1-x)e^x \end{equation*}
    Since \(e^x\gt 0\) for all \(x\text{,}\) the sign of \(H'(x)\) is determined by the sign of \(1-x\text{.}\) So \(H'(x)\gt 0\) when \(x\lt 1\text{,}\) and \(H'(x)\lt 0\) when \(x\gt 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Therefore \(H(x)\) is increasing on \((-\infty,1)\) and decreasing on \((1,\infty)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Part (c)
    🔗
    To find where \(H\) is concave up or down, we need to find the second derivative. We have
    \begin{equation*} H''(x)=((1-x)e^x)' = -e^x+(1-x)e^x=-xe^x \end{equation*}
    Since \(e^x\gt 0\) for all \(x\text{,}\) the sign of \(H''(x)\) is determined by the sign of \(-x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    So \(H''(x)\gt 0\) when \(x\lt 0\text{,}\) and \(H''(x)\lt 0\) when \(x\gt 0\text{.}\) Therefore \(H(x)\) is concave up on \((-\infty,0)\) and concave down on \((0,\infty)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Part (d)
    🔗
    We have the following information about the graph of \(y=H(x)\text{:}\)
    • \(H(0)=0\text{,}\) so the graph passes through the origin.
      🔗
    • \(H(x)\) is increasing on \((-\infty,1)\) and decreasing on \((1,\infty)\text{,}\) so the graph has a local maximum at \(x=1\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • \(H(x)\) is concave up on \((-\infty,0)\) and concave down on \((0,\infty)\text{,}\) so the graph has an inflection point at \(x=0\text{.}\)
      🔗
    Putting this all together, we get the following sketch of the graph of \(y=H(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Now let’s move on to the second part of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, which allows us to evaluate definite integrals from an antiderivative (instead of having to take a limit of a Riemann sum).
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection The Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

    Theorem 4.4.8. Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

    Suppose \(F\) is an antiderivative of a continuous function \(f\) on \([a,b]\text{,}\) so \(F'(x)=f(x)\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b f(x)\,dx \ =\ F(b)-F(a). \end{equation*}
    That is, the integral of a rate of change is the total change.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    By the First Fundamental Theorem, \(I(x)=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt\) is an antiderivative of \(f\text{.}\) Any two antiderivatives differ by a constant, so \(I(x)=F(x)+C\text{.}\) Since \(I(a)=0\text{,}\) we get \(0=F(a)+C\text{,}\) so \(C=-F(a)\text{.}\) Thus \(I(x)=F(x)-F(a)\text{,}\) and evaluating at \(x=b\) gives
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b f(x)\,dx=I(b)=F(b)-F(a) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.4.9.

    Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int_{0}^{1} \left(5+x^2-e^x\right)\,dx\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    An antiderivative is \(F(x)=5x+\frac{1}{3}x^3-e^x\text{.}\) Apply the Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \int_{0}^{1} (5+x^2-e^x)\,dx &=& F(1)-F(0) \\[0.6em] &=& \left(5+\frac{1}{3}-e\right)-\left(0-0-1\right) \\[0.6em] &=& 6+\frac{1}{3}-e. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Remark 4.4.10.
    Another antiderivative is \(F(x)=5x+\frac{1}{3}x^3-e^x+7\text{.}\) See that you get the same answer if you use this antiderivative instead:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \int_{0}^{1} (5+x^2-e^x)\,dx &=& F(1)-F(0) \\[0.6em] &=& \left(5+\frac{1}{3}-e + 7 \right)-\left(0-0-1 +7\right) \\[0.6em] &=& 6+\frac{1}{3}-e. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    No matter the value of the constant, the difference \(F(b)-F(a)\) will always be the same, so we get the same answer for the integral.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 4.4.11.

    The Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus allows us to evaluate a definite integral by finding an antiderivative. In practice, we often write the evaluation of the integral as
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b f(x)\,dx \ =\ F(b)-F(a) \ =\ \left. F(x)\right|_a^b. \end{equation*}
    The notation \(\left. F(x)\right|_a^b\) is read as "F of x evaluated from a to b" and means \(F(b)-F(a)\text{.}\) This is just a convenient shorthand for writing the difference of the antiderivative at the endpoints.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.4.12.

    Determine the net area between \(y=\dfrac{1+\sqrt{x}}{x}\) and the \(x\)-axis on the interval \([1,4]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The net area is given by a definite integral
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \text{net area} &=& \displaystyle \int_1^4 \frac{1+\sqrt{x}}{x}\,dx \\[0.6em] &=& \displaystyle \int_1^4 \frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}}\,dx \\[0.6em] &=& \left. \ln|x|+2\sqrt{x}\right|_1^4 \\[0.6em] &=& \left(\ln(4)+4\right)-\left(\ln(1)+2\right) \\[0.6em] &=& \ln(4)+2 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    So the net area is \(\ln(4)+2\text{.}\)

    Some True/False Problems.

    Determine whether each statement is true or false and briefly justify your reasoning.
    🔗
    1.
    The notation \(\left. F(x)\right|_a^b\) means \(F(a)-F(b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The notation \(\left. F(x)\right|_a^b\) means \(F(b)-F(a)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    \(\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/4} \sec^2(x)~dx = 1\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. An antiderivative of \(\sec^2(x)\) is \(\tan(x)\text{,}\) so evaluating from \(0\) to \(\pi/4\) gives \(\tan(\pi/4)-\tan(0)=1-0=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    \(\displaystyle\int_1^{e} \dfrac{1}{x}~dx \lt \displaystyle\int_0^{1} \dfrac{1}{1+x^2} ~dx \)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Using the Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, we can evaluate both integrals. The left integral evaluates to
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle\int_1^{e} \frac{1}{x}~dx &=& \ln|x|\Bigg|_1^e \\[0.6em] &=& \ln(e)-\ln(1) = 1-0 = 1 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    The right integral evaluates to
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle\int_0^{1} \dfrac{1}{1+x^2} ~dx &=& \left. \arctan(x)\right|_0^1 \\[0.6em] &=& \arctan(1)-\arctan(0) \\[0.6em] &=& \frac{\pi}{4}-0 = \frac{\pi}{4} \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Since we know \(\pi\lt 4\) we have \(\frac{\pi}{4}\lt 1\text{,}\) so \(\displaystyle\int_1^{e} \dfrac{1}{x}~dx \gt \displaystyle\int_0^{1} \dfrac{1}{1+x^2} ~dx \text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    4.
    The area between \(y=5^x\) and the \(x\)-axis on the interval \([0,1]\) is \(5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The area is given by the integral \(\displaystyle\int_0^1 5^x\,dx\text{.}\) An antiderivative of \(5^x\) is \(\frac{5^x}{\ln(5)}\text{,}\) so evaluating from 0 to 1 gives
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle\int_0^1 5^x\,dx &=& \left. \frac{5^x}{\ln(5)}\right|_0^1 \\[0.6em] &=& \frac{5}{\ln(5)}-\frac{1}{\ln(5)} \\[0.6em] &=& \frac{4}{\ln(5)} \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Which is certainly not the same as 5.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    5.
    If \(F(x)=\displaystyle\int_2^x e^{t-2}\,dt\text{,}\) then \(F(2)=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Since the lower and upper limits are the same, \(F(2)=\displaystyle\int_2^2 e^{t-2}\,dt=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    6.
    If \(F'(x)=f(x)\text{,}\) then \(\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) represents the total change in \(F\) from \(x=a\) to \(x=b\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. By the Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, \(\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx=F(b)-F(a)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    7.
    Water flows into a tank at a rate of \(r(t)=1+\sqrt{t}\) liters per minute, where \(t\) is the time in minutes. The total amount of water that flows into the tank in the first 9 minutes is 27 liters.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. The integral \(\displaystyle\int_0^9 r(t)\,dt\) gives the total amount of water that flows into the tank from time \(t=0\) to time \(t=9\) minutes. Evaluate
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle\int_0^9 (1+\sqrt{t})\,dt &=& \displaystyle\int_0^9 1 + t^{1/2}\,dt \\[0.6em] &=& \left. t+\frac{2}{3}t^{3/2}\right|_0^9 \\[0.6em] &=& (9+\frac{2}{3}\cdot 27) - (0+0) \\[0.6em] &=& 27 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    So the total amount of water that flows into the tank in the first 9 minutes is 27 liters.

    🔗
    8.
    If \(F(x)=\displaystyle\int_0^x f(t)\,dt\) and \(f(x)>0\) on an interval, then \(F(x)\) is increasing on that interval.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Since \(F'(x)=f(x)\text{,}\) if \(f(x)>0\text{,}\) then \(F\) is increasing.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    9.
    If \(F(x)=\displaystyle\int_0^x f(t)\,dt\) and \(F(x)>0\) on an interval, then \(f(x)\) is increasing on that interval.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The condition \(F(x)>0\) does not imply that \(f(x)\) is increasing. For example, consider \(F(x)=1+\tfrac13 x^3\) on \((-1,0)\text{.}\) Then \(F(x) >0\text{,}\) and yet \(f(x) = x^2\) is decreasing on \((-1,0)\)
    🔗

    🔗
    10.
    Consider the graph of \(f(t)\) below and let \(F(x)=\displaystyle\int_0^x f(t)\,dt\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Then \(F'(8) \gt F(8)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. We can evaluate \(F(8)\) by finding the signed area under the graph of \(f(t)\) from \(t=0\) to \(t=8\) which is \(5 + (-12)=-7\text{.}\) Since \(F'(8)=f(8)=0\gt -7=F(8)\text{,}\) we have \(F'(8)\gt F(8)\text{.}\)
