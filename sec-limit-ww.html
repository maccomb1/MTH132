    Section 1.6 Additional Worked-Out Problems

    Subsection Section 1.1 Limits from Graphs and Notation

    Example 1.6.1. Graphing a Function to Find a Limit.

    Consider the function \(f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 & \text{ if } x \lt 2 \\ 3 & \text{ if } x = 2 \\ 4 - x & \text{ if } x \gt 2 \end{cases}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Sketch the graph of \(f\) and use it to find \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2} f(x)\text{,}\) if it exists.
    🔗
    Solution.
    To graph \(f\text{,}\) we first graph the pieces of the function on their respective domains. For \(x \lt 2\text{,}\) we graph \(y = x^2\text{,}\) which is a parabola opening upwards. At \(x = 2\text{,}\) we have a single point \((2, 3)\text{.}\) For \(x \gt 2\text{,}\) we graph \(y = 4 - x\text{,}\) which is a line with a negative slope. Together, these pieces form the graph of \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Now, we analyze the behavior of \(f(x)\) as \(x\) approaches 2 from both sides. As \(x\) approaches 2 from the left (\(x \to 2^-\)), \(f(x) = x^2\) approaches \(4\text{.}\) As \(x\) approaches 2 from the right (\(x \to 2^+\)), \(f(x) = 4 - x\) approaches \(2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Since the left-hand limit (\(4\)) and the right-hand limit (\(2\)) are not equal, the overall limit \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2} f(x)\) does not exist.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.6.3.

    Consider the function \(\displaystyle f(x)=\frac{x^2-1}{x^2+3x+2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Find all the vertical asymptotes of \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    To find the vertical asymptotes of the function \(f(x)=\frac{x^2-1}{x^2+3x+2}\text{,}\) we first factor the denominator: \(x^2 + 3x + 2 = (x + 1)(x + 2)\text{.}\) The vertical asymptotes occur where the denominator is zero, provided the numerator is not also zero at those points. Setting the denominator equal to zero gives us: \((x + 1)(x + 2) = 0\text{,}\) which has solutions \(x = -1\) and \(x = -2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Next, we check the numerator at these points: \(x^2 - 1 = (x - 1)(x + 1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    At \(x = -1\text{,}\) the numerator is zero, indicating there is no vertical asymptote there (in fact it will be called a removable discontinuity once we learn about this in our continuity section).
    🔗
    At \(x = -2\text{,}\) the numerator is non-zero (\(3\)), confirming that there is a vertical asymptote at this point.
    🔗
    Therefore, the only vertical asymptote of \(f\) is at \(x = -2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Section 1.2 Limit Laws and Algebraic Techniques

    Example 1.6.4.

    Evaluate the limit: \(\displaystyle \lim_{a \to 0} \frac{(a-3)^2 - 9}{a}.\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Begin by simplifying the expression in the numerator. Expand \((a-3)^2\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} (a-3)^2 - 9 & =& (a^2 - 6a + 9) - 9 \\ &=& a^2 - 6a. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Substituting this back into the limit gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{a \to 0} \frac{a^2 - 6a}{a}. \end{equation*}
    Factor \(a\) from the numerator and simplify:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \lim_{a \to 0} \frac{a(a-6)}{a} & =& \displaystyle \lim_{a \to 0} (a-6). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now evaluate the limit by direct substitution:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{a \to 0} (a-6) = -6. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the value of the limit is \(-6\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.6.5.

    Evaluate the limit: \(\displaystyle \lim_{h \to 1} \frac{\frac{1}{h+1} - \frac{1}{2}}{h-1}.\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Direct substitution gives a meaningless expression \(\frac{0}{0}\text{,}\) so we begin by simplifying the numerator.
    🔗
    Combine the fractions in the numerator using a common denominator:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{1}{h+1} - \frac{1}{2} & =& \displaystyle \frac{2 - (h+1)}{2(h+1)}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Simplify the numerator:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2 - (h+1) = 1 - h. \end{equation*}
    Substitute this back into the original expression:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\frac{1}{h+1} - \frac{1}{2}}{h-1} = \frac{1-h}{2(h+1)(h-1)}. \end{equation*}
    Notice that \(1-h = -(h-1)\text{,}\) so we can factor and cancel:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{1-h}{2(h+1)(h-1)} &=& \displaystyle \frac{-(h-1)}{2(h+1)(h-1)} \\[0.8em] &=& \displaystyle -\frac{1}{2(h+1)}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now we can safely evaluate the limit by substitution:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{h \to 1} -\frac{1}{2(h+1)} = -\frac{1}{4}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{h \to 1} \frac{\frac{1}{h+1} - \frac{1}{2}}{h-1} = -\frac{1}{4}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.6.6.

    Suppose \(f\) is a function such that for all \(x\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \cos(\pi x)\le f(x)\le x^2-4x+5. \end{equation*}
    Use the Squeeze Theorem to evaluate \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 2} f(x)\text{,}\) or explain why the Squeeze Theorem does not apply.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Compute the limits of the bounding functions as \(x\to 2\text{.}\) Since \(\cos(\pi x)\) and polynomials are continuous, we can substitute \(x=2\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 2}\cos(\pi x) &=& \cos(2\pi)=1, \\[0.8em] \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 2}(x^2-4x+5) &=& 2^2-4(2)+5 \\[0.4em] &=& 4-8+5 \\[0.4em] &=& 1. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Both bounding functions approach the same value, \(1\text{.}\) Therefore, by the Squeeze Theorem,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 2} f(x)=1. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Section 1.3 Limits at Infinity: Horizontal Asymptotes

    Example 1.6.7.

    Evaluate the limit: \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{\sqrt{x}-x^{-1}}{x^{3/2}-\sqrt[3]{x}}.\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    To evaluate this limit, we compare the dominant powers of \(x\) in the numerator and denominator. The largest power of \(x\) in the numerator is \(x^{1/2}\text{,}\) and the largest power of \(x\) in the denominator is \(x^{3/2}\text{.}\) Therefore
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) \approx \frac{x^{1/2}}{x^{3/2}} = x^{-1} = \frac{1}{x}. \end{equation*}
    As \(x \to \infty\text{,}\) \(\frac{1}{x} \to 0\text{.}\) Thus, the limit is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{\sqrt{x}-x^{-1}}{x^{3/2}-\sqrt[3]{x}} = 0. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.6.8.

    Evaluate the limits
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{3-2x-8x^3+3x^4}{2x^4+x+1} \quad\text{and}\quad \lim_{x\to -\infty}\frac{3-2x-8x^3+3x^4}{2x^4+x+1}. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    To evaluate limits at infinity for rational functions, we compare the dominant (highest–power) terms in the numerator and denominator. In this case, the dominant term in the numerator is \(3x^4\text{,}\) and the dominant term in the denominator is \(2x^4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For large values of \(|x|\text{,}\) the lower–degree terms become negligible, so the function behaves like
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) \approx \frac{3x^4}{2x^4} = \frac{3}{2}. \end{equation*}
    Since both dominant terms involve \(x^4\text{,}\) which is positive for both positive and negative values of \(x\text{,}\) this approximation applies as \(x\to\infty\) and as \(x\to -\infty\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{3-2x-8x^3+3x^4}{2x^4+x+1} = \lim_{x\to -\infty}\frac{3-2x-8x^3+3x^4}{2x^4+x+1} = \frac{3}{2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.6.9.

    Let \(f(x)=\dfrac{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}{x+1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (a) Evaluate \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) Find the equation of the horizontal asymptote associated with this limit.
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) To evaluate \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)\text{,}\) we analyze the dominant terms in the numerator and denominator. The dominant term in the numerator is \(\sqrt{x^{2}} = x\text{,}\) and the dominant term in the denominator is \(x\text{.}\) Therefore, for large values of \(|x|\text{,}\) the function behaves like
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) \approx \frac{x}{x} = 1. \end{equation*}
    Thus,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty} f(x) = 1. \end{equation*}
    (b) Since \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=1\text{,}\) the curve approaches height \(1\) as \(x\) becomes large. Therefore the horizontal asymptote (for \(x\to\infty\)) is \(y=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.6.10.

    Evaluate the limit: \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty} \left(\sqrt{x^2+x}-x\right).\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Direct substitution leads to a confusing expression of the form \(\infty-\infty\text{.}\) To resolve this and apply our technique, let’s first multiply by the conjugate of the expression.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \sqrt{x^2+x}-x \amp=\amp \displaystyle \left(\sqrt{x^2+x}-x\right) \cdot \frac{\sqrt{x^2+x}+x}{\sqrt{x^2+x}+x} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{(x^2+x)-x^2}{\sqrt{x^2+x}+x} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+x}+x}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    The dominant term in the numerator is \(x\text{,}\) and the dominant term in the denominator is also \(x\text{.}\) Therefore, for large values of \(\sqrt{x^2} = x\text{,}\) the function behaves like
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) \approx \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2} + x} = \frac{x}{x+x} = \frac{1}{2}. \end{equation*}
    Thus,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty} \left(\sqrt{x^2+x}-x\right) = \frac{1}{2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Section 1.4 Continuity

    Example 1.6.11.

    For each function defined below, determine whether it is continuous or discontinuous. Briefly justify your choice.
    🔗
    (a) Let \(f(t)\) be the amount of water (in gallons) remaining in a reservoir after \(t\) hours of steady drainage.
    🔗
    (b) Let \(g(t)\) be the floor number that an elevator is on at time \(t\) seconds.
    🔗
    (c) Let \(h(d)\) be the number of pages read in a book after \(d\) days.
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) Continuous. The function \(f(t)\) is continuous because the amount of water in the reservoir changes smoothly over time as it drains steadily. There are no sudden jumps or breaks in the amount of water; it decreases gradually.
    🔗
    (b) Discontinuous. The function \(g(t)\) is discontinuous because the elevator moves between floors in jumps. The floor number changes suddenly when the elevator passes from one floor to the next.
    🔗
    (c) Discontinuous. The function \(h(d)\) is discontinuous because the number of pages read is a step function. The number of pages read increases in whole numbers, resulting in jumps rather than a smooth change
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.6.12. Assessing and Fixing a Removable Discontinuity.

    Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)= \begin{cases} \dfrac{x-2}{x^2+3x-10}, & x \neq 2,\\[0.6em] 4, & x = 2. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    (a) Determine whether \(f\) is continuous at \(x=2\text{.}\) Justify your answer.
    🔗
    (b) If \(f\) is not continuous at \(x=2\text{,}\) explain how to redefine \(f(2)\) so that \(f\) becomes continuous at \(x=2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) To evaluate the limit as \(x \to 2\text{,}\) we first simplify the expression for \(x \neq 2\text{.}\) Factor the denominator:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x^2 + 3x - 10 = (x+5)(x-2). \end{equation*}
    For \(x \neq 2\text{,}\) the function simplifies to
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{x-2}{(x+5)(x-2)} = \frac{1}{x+5}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 2} f(x) = \frac{1}{2+5} = \frac{1}{7}. \end{equation*}
    However, the value of the function at \(x=2\) is \(f(2) = 4.\) Since the limit exists but is not equal to the function value, \(f\) is not continuous at \(x=2\text{.}\) This is a removable discontinuity.
    🔗
    (b) To remove the discontinuity, we redefine \(f(2)\) to equal the limit:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(2) = \frac{1}{7}. \end{equation*}
    With this definition, \(f\) becomes continuous at \(x=2\text{.}\) The full definition of \(f\) is now
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)= \begin{cases} \dfrac{x-2}{x^2+3x-10}, & x \neq 2,\\[0.6em] \dfrac{1}{7}, & x = 2. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.6.13.

    The graph of a function \(f\) is shown in the figure below.
    🔗
    For each value of \(x\) listed below, determine whether \(f\) is continuous or discontinuous at that point. If \(f\) is discontinuous, classify the type of discontinuity.
    🔗
    (a)
    Classify the behavior of \(f\) at \(x=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    From the graph, as \(x \to 0^-\text{,}\) the function approaches the open circle at height \(2\text{,}\) while as \(x \to 0^+\text{,}\) the function approaches the value \(-1\text{.}\) Since the one-sided limits are not equal, the limit does not exist.
    🔗
    Therefore, \(f\) has a jump discontinuity at \(x=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    Classify the behavior of \(f\) at \(x=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    As \(x \to 1\) from both sides, the graph approaches the same height, indicated by an open circle at \((1,1)\text{.}\) However, there is no filled point at \(x=1\text{,}\) so \(f(1)\) is not defined.
    🔗
    Since the limit exists but \(f(1)\) is undefined, \(f\) has a removable discontinuity at \(x=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (c)
    Classify the behavior of \(f\) at \(x=3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The graph shows a dashed vertical line at \(x=3\text{,}\) and the function values increase without bound as \(x \to 3^+\text{.}\) This indicates that the function grows arbitrarily large near \(x=3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Therefore, \(f\) has an infinite discontinuity (a vertical asymptote) at \(x=3\text{.}\)

    Subsection Section 1.5 Intermediate Value Theorem

    Example 1.6.15.

    A function \(f\) is continuous on \([2,5]\text{,}\) with \(f(2)=7\) and \(f(5)=1\text{.}\) What does the Intermediate Value Theorem guarantee about the equation \(f(x)=4\text{?}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since \(f\) is continuous on the interval \([2,5]\) and \(f(2)=7\) and \(f(5)=1\text{,}\) the Intermediate Value Theorem states that for any value \(k\) between \(f(2)\) and \(f(5)\text{,}\) there exists at least one \(c\) in \((2,5)\) such that \(f(c)=k\text{.}\) Since \(4\) is between \(7\) and \(1\text{,}\) there exists at least one \(c\) in \((2,5)\) such that \(f(c)=4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.6.16.

    Let \(f(x)=\ln(x)-x+2\text{.}\) Use the Intermediate Value Theorem to show that \(f(x)=0\) has at least one solution for \(x>1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, we need to show that \(f\) is continuous for \(x>1\text{.}\) Since \(\ln(x)\) and \(-x+2\) are both continuous for \(x>0\text{,}\) their sum \(f(x)=\ln(x)-x+2\) is also continuous for \(x>0\text{,}\) and thus for \(x>1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Next, we evaluate \(f(1)\) and \(f(4)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} f(1) &=& \ln(1) - 1 + 2 \\ &=& 0 - 1 + 2 \\ &=& 1, \\[1em] f(4) &=& \ln(4) - 4 + 2 \\ &=& \ln(4) - 2 \lt 0 \quad \text{(since } \ln(4) \lt \ln(e^2)\text{)}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Since \(f(1)=1>0\) and \(f(4)<0\text{,}\) by the Intermediate Value Theorem, there exists at least one \(c\) in \((1,4)\) such that \(f(c)=0\text{.}\) Therefore, the equation \(f(x)=0\) has at least one solution for \(x>1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.6.17.

    The temperature \(T(t)\) (in degrees Celsius) at a location varies continuously over time. If \(T(8\text{:}00\text{ am})=12\) and \(T(2\text{:}00\text{ pm})=26\text{,}\) explain why the temperature must have been exactly \(20^\circ\) at some time between 8:00 am and 2:00 pm.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since temperature varies continuously over time, the function \(T(t)\) is continuous on the interval from 8:00 am to 2:00 pm. Given that \(T(8\text{:}00\text{ am})=12\) and \(T(2\text{:}00\text{ pm})=26\text{,}\) by the Intermediate Value Theorem, for any temperature value \(k\) between \(12\) and \(26\text{,}\) there exists at least one time \(c\) in the interval from 8:00 am to 2:00 pm such that \(T(c)=k\text{.}\) Since \(20\) is between \(12\) and \(26\text{,}\) there must be at least one time \(c\) in that interval when the temperature was exactly \(20^\circ\text{.}\)
