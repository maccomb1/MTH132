    Subsection Section 3.1 Motion in Physics

    Example 3.9.1.

    The position of a particle moving along a line is given by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} s(t) = 2t^3 - 9t^2 + 12t, \quad t \ge 0, \end{equation*}
    where \(s\) is measured in meters and \(t\) in seconds.
    🔗
    (a) Find all times when the velocity equals \(0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) Determine whether the particle is moving to the right or to the left at \(t = 3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (c) Find all times when the particle has acceleration \(6 \text{ m/sec}^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) The velocity is the derivative of the position function:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} v(t) = s'(t) = 6t^2 - 18t + 12. \end{equation*}
    Setting the velocity equal to zero gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 6t^2 - 18t + 12 = 0 \implies t^2 - 3t + 2 = 0 \implies (t-1)(t-2) = 0. \end{equation*}
    Thus, the velocity equals zero at \(t = 1\) and \(t = 2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) To determine the direction of motion at \(t = 3\text{,}\) we can evaluate the velocity at \(t = 3\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} v(3) = 6(3)^2 - 18(3) + 12 = 54 - 54 + 12 = 12. \end{equation*}
    Since \(v(3) \geq 0\text{,}\) the particle is moving to the right at \(t = 3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (c) The acceleration is the derivative of the velocity function:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} a(t) = v'(t) = 12t - 18. \end{equation*}
    Setting the acceleration equal to \(6\) gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 12t - 18 = 6 \implies 12t = 24 \implies t = 2. \end{equation*}
    So the particle has acceleration \(6 \text{ m/sec}^2\) at \(t = 2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.9.2.

    A particle moves vertically according to \(s(t)=15t-at^2\text{,}\) where \(s\) is measured in meters, \(t\) in seconds, and \(a\) is a positive constant.
    🔗
    If the particle reaches maximum height at \(t=3\) seconds, find:
    🔗
    (a) the value of \(a\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) the maximum height reached.
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) The velocity is the derivative of the position function:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} v(t) = s'(t) = 15 - 2at. \end{equation*}
    Since the particle reaches maximum height at \(t=3\text{,}\) the velocity must equal zero at \(t=3\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} v(3) = 15 - 2a(3) = 15 - 6a = 0 \implies 6a = 15 \implies a = \frac{5}{2}. \end{equation*}
    (b) To find the maximum height, we can evaluate the position function at \(t=3\) using the value of \(a\) we just found:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} s(3) = 15(3) - \frac{5}{2}(3)^2 = 45 - \frac{5}{2}(9) = 45 - \frac{45}{2} = \frac{45}{2} \end{equation*}
    So the maximum height reached is \(\frac{45}{2}\) meters.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.9.3.

    One of the curves below represents the graph of a position function, and the other curve represents the graph of the corresponding velocity function. Which curve is which?
    🔗
    Solution.
    First consider the possibility that the solid red curve is the graph of \(y=s(t)\text{.}\) If that is the case, then the graph of the derivative (the velocity function) \(y=s'(t)\) must satisfy:
    🔗
    • The graph of \(y=s'(t)\) must pass through \((0,0)\text{,}\) since the solid red curve has a horizontal tangent at \(t=0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The graph of \(y=s'(t)\) must be above the \(t\)-axis for \(t<0\text{,}\) since the solid red curve is increasing there.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The graph of \(y=s'(t)\) must be below the \(t\)-axis for \(t>0\text{,}\) since the solid red curve is decreasing there.
      🔗
      🔗
    Although the dashed blue curve may pass through \((0,0)\text{,}\) the rest of these sign conditions do not match it. Therefore, the solid red curve cannot be \(s(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Next, assume the dashed blue curve is the position function \(y=s(t)\text{.}\) Let \(a\) and \(b\) be the \(t\)-values where the dashed blue curve has its local minimum and local maximum, respectively. Then the derivative (the velocity function) \(y=s'(t)\) must satisfy:
    🔗
    • The graph of \(y=s'(t)\) must cross the \(t\)-axis at \(t=a\) and \(t=b\text{,}\) since the dashed blue curve has horizontal tangents at those points.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The graph of \(y=s'(t)\) must be above the \(t\)-axis on \((a,b)\text{,}\) since the dashed blue curve is increasing there.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The graph of \(y=s'(t)\) must be below the \(t\)-axis for \(t<a\) and for \(t>b\text{,}\) since the dashed blue curve is decreasing on those intervals.
      🔗
      🔗
    This description matches the solid red curve. Therefore, the dashed blue curve is \(y=s(t)\) and the solid red curve is \(y=s'(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.9.4.

    The graph below shows the velocity \(v=f(t)\) of a particle moving along a horizontal line for the open interval \((0,10)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (a) When does the particle move forward?
    🔗
    (b) When does the particle slow down?
    🔗
    (c) When is the particle’s acceleration positive?
    🔗
    (d) When does the particle stand still for more than an instant?
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) The particle moves forward when \(v(t) > 0\text{,}\) meaning the velocity graph is above the \(t\)-axis. From the graph, this occurs on \((2,7)\cup(9,10)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) The particle slows down when its speed \(|v(t)|\) is decreasing. If \(v(t) > 0\) (moving forward), slowing down means \(v(t)\) is decreasing, so \(v'(t) < 0\text{.}\) If \(v(t) < 0\) (moving backward), slowing down means \(v(t)\) is increasing toward zero, so \(v'(t) > 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    From the graph, \(v(t) < 0\) on \((0,2)\) and the velocity is increasing there, so the particle slows down on \((0,2)\text{.}\) Also, \(v(t) > 0\) on \((2,7)\) and the velocity is decreasing on \((6,7)\text{,}\) so the particle slows down on \((6,7)\text{.}\) Therefore, the particle slows down on \((0,2)\cup(6,7)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    At times where the velocity graph has corners, the derivative \(v'(t)\) (acceleration) is not defined, so those times should not be included.
    🔗
    (c) The acceleration is positive when \(v'(t) > 0\text{,}\) meaning the velocity graph has positive slope. From the graph, this occurs on \((0,4)\cup(9,10)\) (excluding any corner points).
    🔗
    (d) The particle stands still when \(v(t)=0\text{.}\) From the graph, this happens at \(t=2\) and for \(t\in[7,9]\text{.}\) Standing still for more than an instant means an interval of time, so the answer is \([7,9]\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Subsection Section 3.2 Related Rates

    Example 3.9.5.

    A computer animator creates a circle for which the area of the circle increases at the rate of \(2 \text{ mm}^2/\text{sec}\text{.}\) Find the rate of change of the circumference of the circle at the time that the radius is \(3\) mm.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(A(t)\text{,}\) \(r(t)\text{,}\) and \(C(t)\) denote the changing area, radius, and circumference of the circle at time \(t\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We are given the rate of change of the area:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle A'(t)=2 \ \text{mm}^2/\text{sec}. \end{equation*}
    We are also told that at a particular time, say \(t=0\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} r(0)=3 \ \text{mm}. \end{equation*}
    Our goal is to determine \(\displaystyle C'(0).\)
    🔗
    The circumference of a circle is related to the radius by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} C(t)=2\pi r(t). \end{equation*}
    Differentiating both sides with respect to \(t\) gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} C'(t)=2\pi r'(t). \end{equation*}
    Therefore, at time \(t^\*\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} C'(0)=2\pi r'(0). \end{equation*}
    To determine \(r'(0)\text{,}\) we relate the radius to the area. The area of a circle is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A(t)=\pi r^2(t). \end{equation*}
    Differentiating with respect to \(t\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A'(t)=2\pi r(t) r'(t). \end{equation*}
    Solve for \(r'(t)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} r'(t)=\frac{A'(t)}{2\pi r(t)}. \end{equation*}
    Substitute the known values at \(t=0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} r'(0)= \frac{2}{2\pi \cdot 3} = \frac{1}{3\pi} \ \text{mm/sec}. \end{equation*}
    Now substitute this into the expression for \(C'(0)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} C'(0) = 2\pi\left(\frac{1}{3\pi}\right) = \frac{2}{3} \ \text{mm/sec}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, when the radius is \(3\) mm, the circumference is increasing at a rate of \(\frac{2}{3}\) mm/sec.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.9.6.

    A hot air balloon is released from its launching area and watched by a crowd in a viewing area \(100\) feet away from the launch site. What is the vertical speed of the balloon when its distance from the viewing area is \(300\) feet and that distance is increasing at a rate of \(600\) ft/hr?
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(b(t)\) denote the changing height of the balloon and let \(c(t)\) denote the changing distance from the balloon to the viewing area.
    🔗
    At a particular instant, say \(t=0\text{,}\) we are given
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} c(0) = 300 \text{ ft}, \qquad c'(0) = 600 \text{ ft/hr}. \end{equation*}
    We want to determine the vertical speed of the balloon at that time:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle b'(0). \end{equation*}
    The viewing area, launch site, and balloon form a right triangle. The base of the triangle is \(100\) ft, the height is \(b(t)\text{,}\) and the hypotenuse is \(c(t)\text{.}\) By the Pythagorean Theorem,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 100^2 + b^2(t) = c^2(t). \end{equation*}
    Differentiate both sides with respect to \(t\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2b(t)b'(t) = 2c(t)c'(t). \end{equation*}
    Solving for \(b'(t)\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} b'(t) = \frac{c(t)c'(t)}{b(t)}. \end{equation*}
    To evaluate this at \(t=0\text{,}\) we still need the value of \(b(0)\text{.}\) Using the original relationship,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 100^2 + b^2(0) = c^2(0) = 300^2. \end{equation*}
    Solve for the height:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} b(0) = \sqrt{300^2 - 100^2} = \sqrt{80000}. \end{equation*}
    Now substitute the known quantities into the formula for \(b'(0)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} b'(0) = \frac{300 \cdot 600}{\sqrt{80000}}. \end{equation*}
    Simplifying,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} b'(0) = \frac{180000}{200\sqrt{2}} = 450\sqrt{2} \text{ ft/hr}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, when the balloon is \(300\) feet from the viewing area, its vertical speed is \(450\sqrt{2}\) ft/hr.
    🔗

    Example 3.9.7.

    A spotlight is on the ground \(20\) ft away from a wall and a \(6\) ft tall person is walking toward the wall at a rate of \(\frac{5}{2}\) ft/sec.
    🔗
    How fast is the height of the shadow changing when the person is \(8\) ft from the wall?
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let
    🔗
    • \(x(t)\) be the distance of the person from the spotlight,
      🔗
    • \(z(t)\) be the distance of the person from the wall, and
      🔗
    • \(y(t)\) be the height of the shadow on the wall.
      🔗
    Because the wall is \(20\) ft from the spotlight,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z(t)=20-x(t). \end{equation*}
    The person is walking toward the wall at a rate of \(\frac{5}{2}\) ft/sec, so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z'(t)=-\frac{5}{2}. \end{equation*}
    At the instant of interest,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z(0)=8, \qquad x(0)=20-8=12. \end{equation*}
    We want to determine the rate of change of the height of the shadow:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'(0). \end{equation*}
    From the geometry of the situation there are two similar triangles: the large triangle formed by the spotlight and the shadow on the wall, and the smaller triangle formed by the spotlight and the person. Using similar triangles,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{y(t)}{6}=\frac{20}{x(t)}. \end{equation*}
    Solving for \(y(t)\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(t)=\frac{120}{x(t)}. \end{equation*}
    Differentiate with respect to \(t\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'(t)=-\frac{120}{x^2(t)}\,x'(t). \end{equation*}
    To determine \(x'(t)\text{,}\) differentiate the relationship \(x(t)=20-z(t)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x'(t)=-z'(t). \end{equation*}
    Since \(z'(t)=-\frac{5}{2}\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x'(t)=\frac{5}{2}. \end{equation*}
    Now evaluate the derivative when \(x(0)=12\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'(0) = -\frac{120}{12^2}\left(\frac{5}{2}\right) = -\frac{25}{12}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore the height of the shadow is changing at a rate of \(-\frac{25}{12}\) ft/sec. The negative sign indicates that the height of the shadow is decreasing at that moment.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.9.8.

    A flexible cylinder has been built which can change its volume by changing its height or its radius. However, design constraints require that its height is always twice its radius.
    🔗
    Find the rate of change of the volume of the cylinder when its radius is \(3\) feet and the radius is increasing at a rate of \(2\) ft/min.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(r(t)\) denote the changing radius of the cylinder, \(h(t)\) denote the changing height, and \(V(t)\) denote the volume.
    🔗
    We are given the design constraint
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} h(t)=2r(t). \end{equation*}
    At a particular time, say \(t=0\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} r(0)=3, \qquad r'(0)=2. \end{equation*}
    We want to determine the rate of change of the volume at that same time:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle V'(0). \end{equation*}
    The volume of a cylinder is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} V(t)=\pi r^2(t)h(t). \end{equation*}
    Using the relationship \(h(t)=2r(t)\text{,}\) we can rewrite the volume in terms of the radius alone:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} V(t)=\pi r^2(t)\bigl(2r(t)\bigr)=2\pi r^3(t). \end{equation*}
    Differentiate with respect to \(t\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} V'(t)=6\pi r^2(t)r'(t). \end{equation*}
    Now evaluate this derivative at \(t=0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} V'(0) = 6\pi (3)^2(2) = 108\pi. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, when the radius is \(3\) ft, the volume is increasing at a rate of
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 108\pi \text{ ft}^3/\text{min}. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.9.9.

    A factory produces solar panels according to the production function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} S = 600L^{0.3}K^{0.6}, \end{equation*}

    (a) How many panels are produced when \(L=100\) and \(K=81\text{?}\)

    (b) The company keeps equipment spending fixed at \(K=81\text{.}\) At what rate is production changing with respect to labor spending when \(L=100\text{?}\)

    Solution.
    Part (a)
    🔗
    Substitute the given values into the production function.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} S = 600(100)^{0.3}(81)^{0.6}. \end{equation*}
    Without a calculator we would leave the answer in this form, but since this is an example exercise we can compute the powers to get a numerical approximation.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} S \approx 33361. \end{equation*}
    Thus the factory produces approximately \(33361\) solar panels.

    Part (b)
    🔗
    Since equipment spending is fixed at \(K=81\text{,}\) we can plug that in, leaving our only variable \(L\text{.}\)

    Therefore production is increasing at approximately \(100\) panels per additional thousand dollars spent on labor.

    Subsection Section 3.3 Linear Approximation and L’Hopital’s Rule

    Example 3.9.10.

    The equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} e^x + \sin x = 1.1 \end{equation*}
    has a solution near \(x=0\text{.}\) Approximate this solution by replacing the left-hand side with its linearization at \(x=0\) and then solving the resulting equation.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=e^x+\sin x. \end{equation*}
    We approximate \(f(x)\) near \(x=0\) using its linearization
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} L(x)=f(0)+f'(0)x. \end{equation*}
    First compute the value of the function at \(x=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(0)=e^0+\sin(0)=1. \end{equation*}
    Next compute the derivative.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x)=e^x+\cos x. \end{equation*}
    Evaluate the derivative at \(x=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(0)=e^0+\cos(0)=1+1=2. \end{equation*}
    Therefore the linearization of \(f(x)\) at \(x=0\) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} L(x)=1+2x. \end{equation*}
    We now approximate the equation \(e^x+\sin x=1.1\) by solving
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 1+2x=1.1. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 2x=0.1. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=0.05. \end{equation*}
    Thus an approximate solution to the equation is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x\approx 0.05. \end{equation*}
    If we have access to technology, we can check how good this approximation is by evaluating the original expression \(e^x+\sin x\) at \(x=0.05\text{.}\) Doing so gives a value very close to \(1.1\text{,}\) confirming that the linear approximation produced a reasonable estimate.
    🔗
    While this type of numerical check can be helpful for building intuition and verifying results, it is important to note that calculators may not be available on quizzes or exams. In those situations the goal is simply to use linearization to produce a reasonable approximation based on calculus rather than numerical computation.

    Example 3.9.11.

    A phone’s battery range \(R(T)\) (in hours) depends on temperature \(T\) (in degrees Fahrenheit). A test reports \(R(70)=11.5\) and \(R(80)=10.8\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (a) Estimate \(R'(70)\) using the average rate of change from \(T=70\) to \(T=80\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) Write the linear approximation to \(R(T)\) near \(T=70\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (c) Use your linear approximation to estimate \(R(74)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (d) Interpret the meaning of \(R'(70)\) in a sentence, including units.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Part (a): Use the slope of the secant line from \(T=70\) to \(T=80\) as an estimate for \(R'(70)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle R'(70)\approx \frac{R(80)-R(70)}{80-70} =\frac{10.8-11.5}{10} =\frac{-0.7}{10} =-0.07. \end{equation*}
    So \(R'(70)\approx -0.07\) hours per degree Fahrenheit.
    🔗
    Part (b): The linearization (tangent line approximation) at \(T=70\) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle L(T)=R(70)+R'(70)(T-70). \end{equation*}
    Using \(R(70)=11.5\) and our estimate \(R'(70)\approx -0.07\text{,}\) we get
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle L(T)=11.5-0.07(T-70). \end{equation*}
    Part (c): Estimate \(R(74)\) by evaluating \(L(74)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle R(74) \amp \approx \amp L(74)\\ \amp \approx \amp 11.5-0.07(74-70)\\ \amp \approx \amp 11.5-0.07(4)\\ \amp \approx \amp 11.5-0.28\\ \amp \approx \amp 11.22. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    So the battery range at \(74^\circ\)F is approximately \(11.22\) hours.
    🔗
    Part (d): The value \(R'(70)\approx -0.07\) hours per \(^\circ\)F means that near \(70^\circ\)F, increasing the temperature by \(1^\circ\)F decreases the battery range by about \(0.07\) hours (about \(4.2\) minutes).

    Subsection Section 3.4 Extrema

    Example 3.9.12.

    Find all critical numbers of the function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\sqrt{x}+\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}}. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Recall that the critical numbers of a function are the values of \(x\) in the domain of \(f\) for which either \(f'(x)=0\) or \(f'(x)\) is undefined.
    🔗
    Rewrite the function using exponents:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=x^{1/2}+x^{-1/2}. \end{equation*}
    Differentiate:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} f'(x) \amp= \frac{1}{2}x^{-1/2}-\frac{1}{2}x^{-3/2} \\ \amp= \frac{1}{2x^{1/2}}-\frac{1}{2x^{3/2}}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Finding a common denominator gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x)=\frac{x-1}{2x^{3/2}}. \end{equation*}
    To determine where \(f'(x)=0\text{,}\) set the numerator equal to zero:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x-1=0. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=1. \end{equation*}
    Next determine where the derivative is undefined by examining the denominator. The denominator is zero when
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2x^{3/2}=0. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=0. \end{equation*}
    However, the original function \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}+1/\sqrt{x}\) is not defined at \(x=0\text{,}\) so this value is not in the domain of \(f\) and therefore cannot be a critical number.
    🔗
    Thus the only critical number of the function is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x=1. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.9.13.

    Find all critical numbers of the function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\frac{2x}{(x^2+2)^2}. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Recall that the critical numbers of a function are the values of \(x\) in the domain of \(f\) for which either \(f'(x)=0\) or \(f'(x)\) is undefined.
    🔗
    We begin by computing the derivative using the quotient rule.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} f'(x) \amp = \frac{(x^2+2)^2(2)-2x\left[2(x^2+2)(2x)\right]}{(x^2+2)^4} \\ \amp = \frac{2(x^2+2)^2-8x^2(x^2+2)}{(x^2+2)^4}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Factor the numerator.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} f'(x) \amp = \frac{(x^2+2)\bigl[2(x^2+2)-8x^2\bigr]}{(x^2+2)^4} \\ \amp = \frac{2(2-3x^2)}{(x^2+2)^3}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    To find where \(f'(x)=0\text{,}\) we set the numerator equal to zero:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2(2-3x^2)=0. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 2-3x^2=0. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x^2=\frac{2}{3}. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=\pm\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}. \end{equation*}
    Next we determine where the derivative is undefined by examining the denominator. The denominator is zero when
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (x^2+2)^3=0. \end{equation*}
    However, \(x^2+2>0\) for all real numbers \(x\text{,}\) so the denominator can never be zero. Therefore \(f'(x)\) is defined for all real \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Thus the critical numbers of \(f(x)\) are
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x=\pm\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.9.15.

    Consider the function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\frac{2}{3}x^3-2x^2-6x+1, \qquad -2 \le x \le 1. \end{equation*}
    Determine the absolute maximum and minimum values of \(f\) and the \(x\)-values where these extrema occur.
    🔗
    Solution.
    First determine the critical numbers of \(f\text{.}\) Compute the derivative.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} f'(x) \amp= \frac{2}{3}\cdot 3x^2 -4x -6 \\ \amp= 2x^2-4x-6 \\ \amp= 2(x^2-2x-3) \\ \amp= 2(x-3)(x+1). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Setting \(f'(x)=0\) gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2(x-3)(x+1)=0. \end{equation*}
    Thus the critical numbers are
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x=-1, \quad x=3. \end{equation*}
    To determine the absolute extrema on the interval \([-2,1]\text{,}\) we evaluate \(f\) at the endpoints and at any critical numbers inside the interval. Since \(x=3\) lies outside the interval, we only check \(x=-2\text{,}\) \(x=1\text{,}\) and \(x=-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} f(-2) \amp= \frac{2}{3}(-2)^3-2(-2)^2-6(-2)+1 = -\frac{1}{3} \\[6pt] f(1) \amp= \frac{2}{3}(1)^3-2(1)^2-6(1)+1 = -\frac{19}{3} \\[6pt] f(-1) \amp= \frac{2}{3}(-1)^3-2(-1)^2-6(-1)+1 = \frac{13}{3}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Comparing these values, the absolute maximum value of \(f\) is \(\frac{13}{3},\) which occurs at \(x=-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The absolute minimum value of \(f\) is \(-\frac{19}{3},\) which occurs at \(x=1\text{.}\)

    Example 3.9.16.

    Consider the function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=|x-2|, \qquad 0 \le x \le 5. \end{equation*}
    Determine the absolute maximum and minimum values of \(f\) and the \(x\)-values where these extrema occur.
    🔗
    Solution.
    When a function involves absolute values, it is often helpful to rewrite it using the definition of absolute value:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |A|= \begin{cases} -A, & A\lt 0 \\ A, & A\ge 0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    For this function,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=|x-2|= \begin{cases} -(x-2), & x\lt 2 \\ x-2, & x\ge 2 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Taking derivatives of each piece gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x)= \begin{cases} -1, & x\lt 2 \\ 1, & x\gt 2 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    The derivative at \(x=2\) is undefined. Indeed,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(2)= \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{|(2+h)-2|-|2-2|}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{|h|}{h}, \end{equation*}
    and this limit does not exist. Thus the only critical number is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x=2. \end{equation*}
    To determine the absolute extrema on the interval \([0,5]\text{,}\) evaluate \(f\) at the critical number and the endpoints.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} f(0) \amp= |0-2| = 2 \\ f(2) \amp= |2-2| = 0 \\ f(5) \amp= |5-2| = 3 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the absolute maximum value of \(f\) is \(3\text{,}\) which occurs at \(x=5\text{.}\) The absolute minimum value of \(f\) is \(0\text{,}\) which occurs at \(x=2\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 3.9.17.

    Consider the function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=x+3x^{2/3}, \qquad -1 \le x \le 1. \end{equation*}
    Determine the absolute maximum and minimum values of \(f\) and the \(x\)-values where these extrema occur.
    🔗
    Solution.
    First determine the critical numbers of \(f\text{.}\) Compute the derivative.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} f'(x) \amp= 1 + 3\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)x^{-1/3} \\ \amp= 1 + 2x^{-1/3} \\ \amp= 1 + \frac{2}{x^{1/3}}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    To determine where \(f'(x)=0\text{,}\) solve
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 1+\frac{2}{x^{1/3}}=0. \end{equation*}
    Multiply both sides by \(x^{1/3}\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x^{1/3}+2=0. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x^{1/3}=-2. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=(-2)^3=-8. \end{equation*}
    Next determine where the derivative is undefined. The derivative \(f'(x)=1+\frac{2}{x^{1/3}}\) is undefined when
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x^{1/3}=0. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=0. \end{equation*}
    Thus the critical numbers are \(x=-8\) and \(x=0\text{.}\) However, only \(x=0\) lies in the interval \([-1,1]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To determine the absolute extrema on \([-1,1]\text{,}\) evaluate \(f\) at the endpoints and the critical number in the interval.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} f(-1) \amp= -1 + 3(-1)^{2/3} = -1 + 3(1) = 2 \\[6pt] f(1) \amp= 1 + 3(1)^{2/3} = 1 + 3 = 4 \\[6pt] f(0) \amp= 0 + 3(0)^{2/3} = 0. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Comparing these values, the absolute maximum value of \(f\) is \(4\text{,}\) which occurs at \(x=1\text{.}\) The absolute minimum value of \(f\) is \(0\text{,}\) which occurs at \(x=0\text{.}\)
